## Overview

Alwa Glebe is a German singer-songwriter and musician known for her distinctive dark, introspective style and her uncompromising artistic vision. Her work, often described as belonging to the realm of dark chanson or experimental art music, combines sophisticated musical composition with lyrically dense and philosophically engaged songwriting.

Glebe has been remarkably candid about her migraine experiences and their relationship to her artistic practice. In her music video for "Will-o'-the-wisps" (*Irrlichter*, 2005), she created "a subconscious visual and musical representation of the migraine from which I suffered at that time." The piece combines haunting vocal delivery, evocative imagery, and a soundscape that captures something of migraine's disorienting intensity. Rather than explaining migraine intellectually, Glebe uses the resources of music and visual art to embody the experience directly.

In her correspondence with migraine researcher Klaus Podoll, Glebe articulated a philosophy of migraine that reflects deep engagement with both the neurological and spiritual dimensions of the condition. She described handling migraine in two ways: first, by coping with pain and finding intuitive insight during attacks—a practice she connected to the spontaneous creation of song during an acute episode; and second, by reflecting on the experience afterward, placing individual migraine episodes within "a much larger context, not only seen as an isolated disease." This dual approach—immediate creative response and subsequent philosophical reflection—characterizes her relationship to migraine as an artist.

Glebe has also drawn explicit connections between her own engagement with migraine and the philosophical reflections of Friedrich Nietzsche, another artist who lived with chronic migraines and found in that suffering a source of both torment and unusual clarity. She emphasizes that pain can be "turned into a stimulating and knowledge bringing experience," suggesting that migraine, when encountered with awareness and openness rather than pure resistance, can become a path to deeper understanding of oneself and one's art. Her work demonstrates that migraine need not silence an artist's voice but can instead become integrated into the very texture and content of their creative expression.
