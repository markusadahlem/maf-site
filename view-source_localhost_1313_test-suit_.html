<p>This curious test is part of the MigraineBrainRadar™ and reveals how your brain makes sense of vague or noisy images — a phenomenon called pareidolia. It’s part of a growing set of perception tasks designed to explore how your brain processes the world, especially during migraine-prone phases.</p>

<h2>A Toolkit Informed by Science, Experience, and Openness<span class="hx-absolute -hx-mt-20" id="a-toolkit-informed-by-science-experience-and-openness"></span>
<a href="#a-toolkit-informed-by-science-experience-and-openness" class="subheading-anchor" aria-label="Permalink for this section"></a></h2><p>Our tools combine scientific knowledge of how the brain works with the lived experience of people who have migraine. We believe that open, exploratory work — shared transparently and refined over time — is essential to uncovering new ways of understanding and managing migraine. The Jesus-in-Toast Task is just one example of this approach.</p>
<hr>
<h2>Your Voice Enters Research<span class="hx-absolute -hx-mt-20" id="your-voice-enters-research"></span>
<a href="#your-voice-enters-research" class="subheading-anchor" aria-label="Permalink for this section"></a></h2><p>We believe that people living with migraine shouldn&rsquo;t just be the subject of research — they should help shape it. That’s why our approach is rooted in Patient and Public Involvement (PPI): research carried out <em>with</em>, not <em>for</em>, those who experience migraine.</p>
<p>Through this model, your experiences help define what questions are asked, how tools are developed, and what outcomes matter. It’s a form of citizen science in medicine — and you can be part of it.
